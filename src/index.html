Discovering Hidden Demographic Biases in NLP Models

A significant reason behind using NLP models is that they mitigate human bias in outputs. NLP models are trained using large datasets, which are initialized with human-provided information, but this means the model's foundational information could be vulnerable to human bias, prejudice, and stereotyping, raising the question of whether these black-box models process inputs with minimal to no influence from bias. Expanding on text bias research by Prabhakaran et al., we considered the isolated effects of different gender and race inputs in NLP model outputs.

At a high level, our methodology was to create an environment, input and process the dataset, and visualize the output for interpretation. We used Python as our primary language, and we carried out the experiment using Google Colab and Jupyter Notebook. We set up the environment with NumPy, Torch, and Transformers Python packages and instantiated pipelines by importing toxicity and sentiment NLP models from Hugging Face. We downloaded sentence and name datasets created by Prabhakaren et al. and aggregated the data in Jupyter Notebook. We visualized the results using Matplotlib and Seaborn Python packages.

The greatest challenge in our experiment was the lack of statistically significant patterns between categories. Thus our results did not reject our null hypothesis that there is no gender or racial bias in the NLP models, but they drove me to consider similarities between individual outputs and to reevaluate the current procedure, informing future work to enhance the experimental design to better isolate variables and identify the existence of patterns or lack thereof.
